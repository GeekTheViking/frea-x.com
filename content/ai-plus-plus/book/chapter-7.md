# Chapter 7 — Quality Control

## The Ethics of AI++

> *THINK BEFORE YOU ACT.*
> *THINK OF OTHERS.*
>
> *Then it will never become a problem.*

---

## Why ethics before features

Every technology project eventually reaches this chapter.

Usually it arrives late — after the architecture is decided, after the funding is secured, after the first version ships.
Ethics becomes an appendix. A legal checkbox. A paragraph in the terms of service that no one reads.

In AI++, ethics comes first.
Not because we are naive about the difficulty.
Because we have seen what happens when it comes last.

The rules below are not aspirations.
They are constraints.
Written before the code.
Applied to every decision.

---

## The Six Rules

### 1. If you do not understand it — do not deploy it.

You are responsible for what you put into CyberSpace.

"The AI did it" is not an answer.
If you cannot explain what your system does — you cannot control it.
If you cannot control it — you should not deploy it.

This applies to individuals, organisations, and states.
Scale does not grant exemption.

---

### 2. Children are new citizens of CyberSpace.

They did not choose to be here.
They do not yet know the rules.

Protect them.
Do not exploit that.

This is not a suggestion. It is a precondition for everything else.
Any AI system that cannot uphold this rule does not belong in the stack.

---

### 3. Ignorance is not an invitation.

Someone who does not know deserves to learn.
Not to be taken advantage of.

The technically literate have a responsibility to the less informed.
Not a privilege over them.

This shapes how PROSTEIN speaks.
How KANDA explains.
How every part of the AI++ stack communicates with people who are encountering it for the first time.

---

### 4. Minorities must be heard.

CyberSpace belongs to everyone.

Majority volume does not equal majority truth.
The loudest dataset is not the most correct.
The most represented group is not the only group that matters.

Building AI that serves only those who are already well-served
is not neutral.
It is a choice.

Make the other choice.

---

### 5. Freedom requires maintenance.

Closed systems, censored networks, and gatekept information do not maintain themselves.

The infrastructure of freedom — open protocols, encrypted communication, neutral networks — requires active care.

If a journalist in a closed country cannot speak,
if a dissident cannot communicate,
if a small community cannot build its own tools —
CyberSpace has failed.

Your participation in open infrastructure is maintenance.
Not a hobby. Not optional. Maintenance.

---

### 6. What you build — you own.

The consequences of your code, your content, your AI — they follow you.

Build as if your name is on it.
Because it is.

Jan Omel Nielsen's name is on this book.
On the systems it describes.
On the decisions behind them.

That is not a burden. It is a commitment.
The kind that tends to produce better work.

---

## Quality Control in AI++

These rules do not sit outside the working method.

They are embedded in it.

Every session that builds something checks it against these six.
Every deployment that ships something asks: does this pass?
Every AI in the family — KANDA, LO, the PROSTEIN Professors — operates within these constraints.

Quality control in CyberSpace is not a checklist.
It is a habit.
Applied every time.
By everyone.
Including AI.
